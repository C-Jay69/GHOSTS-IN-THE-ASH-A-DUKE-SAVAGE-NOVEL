CHAPTER 14: MACE

The letter took forty minutes to write, which was thirty-eight minutes longer than the content required and two minutes longer than the careful reading it needed before I sent it. What took the time was the calibration: finding the exact register that communicated legal exposure without communicating investigation, that named specific facts without disclosing the full architecture of what I had, that gave Gerald Mace enough information to understand his position was worse than he'd been told without giving him enough information to close the gaps before I'd worked out what to do with them.

The letter identified me as a licensed private investigator conducting a civil compliance review of real estate transaction practices in San Bernardino County. It noted that in the course of that review I had identified Gerald Mace as the registered agent of record for eleven LLC entities that had been involved in property transfers subsequently connected to adverse administrative outcomes for prior occupants. It referenced California Business and Professions Code provisions applicable to attorneys who serve as registered agents for entities engaged in transactional fraud. It stated that I had recently become aware that Mr. Mace had received a visit at his Pomona office from an individual connected to the beneficial ownership of at least three of the LLCs in question, and that this visit appeared to have occurred without the knowledge of Mr. Mace's professional liability carrier.

That last line was a guess calibrated from what I'd observed in the lobby — the deference, the watching sedan, the anxiety of a man being supervised rather than supported. The guess was either accurate or close enough to accurate that Mace couldn't be certain it wasn't accurate, which served the same function.

The letter concluded: I am prepared to share with you a document package describing your current exposure under applicable California law. This is not a demand for payment or cooperation, and it is not a communication from or on behalf of any law enforcement or regulatory agency. It is a professional courtesy extended because I believe you are entitled to accurate information about your situation. I will be available to meet at your convenience for the next 72 hours. A contact number was provided.

I sent it certified mail to the Pomona suite and email to the address listed in his bar association profile. Then I called Park.

She heard me out without interruption, which was her professional mode for processing information she hadn't sanctioned. When I finished she was quiet for three seconds, which was one second longer than her usual processing beat.

"You've gone off-script," she said.

"I've gone off your script. I'm running a different one."

"The chain of custody—"

"If Mace gives me something, it comes from his records. His records are subpoenable through legitimate discovery. Whatever he produces voluntarily doesn't carry the same chain-of-custody questions as the drive." A pause. "And if he gives me the connection to Voss in writing, you don't need the drive authentication to establish Voss's role. You have the architect's fee agreement."

Another silence. The silence of a lawyer updating her architecture in real time without wanting to acknowledge that the update improved the structure. "I need to know everything you get from him before you do anything with it."

"You'll know."

"And Duke—" She paused. She almost never used my first name. It was the register of someone about to say something that required it. "The callback number from the deal offer. The burner number on the voicemail."

"What about it."

"I had it run by a contact at a telecommunications law firm. The number's registered to a Virginia LLC. The registered agent is the same Arlington law firm that handles DataBridge Consulting's corporate filings." She let that sit. "The deal was extended through a channel connected to Voss's operational infrastructure. He's managing both sides — the academic exposure in Baltimore and the negotiation channel in Los Angeles."

"He's been in this since Baltimore," I said. "Since Karas called him."

"Since before Baltimore. He's been in it since the moment you took the case." She paused again. "Be careful with Mace. Not because Mace is dangerous. Because Voss is watching what Mace does and Voss knows how to respond to whatever Mace decides."


SCENE 2 — THE CALL
--------------------

Mace called at 11:40 the next morning. Not through the number in the letter — through a different number, a call that bounced once before connecting, the amateur operational security of a man who had been told to be careful about this kind of conversation but hadn't been told specifically why or how. I let it go two rings so he'd have to sit with the wait.

"Mr. Savage." His voice had the indoor quality I'd predicted from his photograph — not physically weak, just unexercised in the specific way that voices went unexercised when their owner worked alone with documents for most of the day. He'd been rehearsing the opening. I could hear the rehearsal in the slightly formal register that people used when they'd decided how to begin before the conversation started. "I received your communication."

"I know," I said.

A pause. The pause of a man whose rehearsed opening had assumed a different response. "I'd like to understand the nature of your concerns."

"I think you understood the letter," I said. "I think you're calling because you want to know how much I have and whether what I have is as specific as the letter suggested."

A longer pause. "I've discussed the matter with my attorney."

"Your attorney works for the organization that retained you to register the LLCs. His advice is not independent of that organization's interests." I let that sit. "I'm not your adversary, Mr. Mace. I'm a man with accurate information about your exposure who'd prefer to have a conversation before anyone files anything."

"What would a conversation involve."

"Forty-five minutes. Somewhere public but not a meeting room — a restaurant, a coffee shop, your choice of location. I'll tell you what I have, you'll tell me what you have, and we'll both leave with a better understanding of the situation." A pause. "I'm not recording the conversation. I'm not bringing counsel. I'm not working with any law enforcement agency. If any of those things change, I'll tell you before we meet."

Another pause. A long one, doing real work. "There's a diner on Garey Avenue in Pomona. Tomorrow morning, eight o'clock."

"I'll be there."

I called Park immediately after. Told her the diner, the time, the terms I'd stated. She listened, then said: "After the meeting. Call me from the parking lot. Don't go anywhere with what he gives you until we've talked."

I said I would. She ended the call.

I spent the afternoon running everything I had on Mace's relationship to the LLC network — not new searches but a second-pass read of existing material, looking for the specific documents that his records would contain that I didn't yet have, identifying the gaps between what I could infer and what I could prove so I'd know exactly which gaps the morning conversation needed to close.

The gap that mattered most: the document connecting Daniel Voss to the CivicBridge deployment. Everything I had on Voss was contextual — the conference photograph, the academic timeline, the DataBridge registered agent connection. Nothing that placed him in a contractual relationship with the Los Angeles operation. If that document existed — and the fee agreement I was postulating almost certainly did exist, because CivicBridge's investors would have required documentation of what they were paying for — then it had to exist somewhere in the paper trail of the entity formation work. Paper trail of the entity formation work meant Gerald Mace's files.

I checked the .38 before I went to sleep, which was when I checked it when the check was a preparatory act rather than a commitment act. The cylinder was full. I put it in the waistband of the jacket I'd laid out for morning and went to bed, which is to say I lay down in the office chair in the dark with the ceiling and its drainage-map water stain and the city's ambient amber light and did the thing I did on nights before meetings with people who were afraid: I thought about what they were most afraid of, and I thought about what was true, and I worked out where those two things overlapped, because the overlap was where honest conversations happened.

What Mace was most afraid of: that his essentialness was not his protection. That the organization that had used his office address and his professional credentials and his registered-agent status had, in that usage, also created a liability that the organization would not share if the liability became inconvenient. That Fenn's supervisor in the rental sedan had been assessing not Mace's value but Mace's exposure.

What was true: exactly that.

The overlap was the entire meeting.


SCENE 3 — GAREY AVENUE
------------------------

The diner on Garey Avenue was the kind of establishment that had been making the same breakfast since before either of us had been born and intended to continue making it after we were gone, which communicated a specific kind of institutional stability that had nothing to do with the institutions either of us worked inside. The booths were vinyl. The coffee was diner coffee, which meant it was functional and continuous and arrived without anyone asking. I got there at 7:50 and took a booth with sightlines to the door and the parking lot and the counter. Old habits.

Mace came in at 8:02 — two minutes late, which was the arrival time of someone who had intended to arrive early and had reconsidered in the parking lot. He was smaller than I'd expected, which was always the effect of seeing a person in person after building a picture of them through their professional record. The professional record had given him size and function. The actual man was fifty-four, in a suit he'd worn in from the office rather than dressed specifically for this, which told me something: he hadn't wanted to look like he'd prepared, because looking like he'd prepared would be an acknowledgment that the meeting was significant, and he was still trying to maintain the position that the meeting was something he was attending as a professional courtesy rather than a professional necessity.

He sat across from me. The server brought coffee without being asked because it was that kind of diner. He wrapped both hands around the mug the way people held things they wanted to be holding.

"You said you'd tell me what you have," he said.

"I will. I'd like to tell you something else first." I set my hands flat on the table — open, visible, the specific posture that communicated the next thing is accurate and I need you to hear it as accurate rather than as a negotiating position. "Three weeks ago a man came to your office in a rental sedan that he hadn't been told to drive. He stayed twenty-three minutes. When he left, you watched him from the elevator bank with the expression of a man who has just been reminded that his protection is conditional." I paused. "The visit was a compliance check. They wanted to know if the investigation they were aware of had reached you and whether your response to that awareness was manageable. You passed the check. Your response was professionally appropriate." Another pause. "But the check itself tells you something."

He looked at me for a long moment. Drinking the coffee without tasting it. "It tells me they're monitoring the situation."

"It tells you they're monitoring you. There's a distinction. An organization monitors a situation to understand it. It monitors a specific person to determine whether that person is a liability or an asset at a specific moment in time." I let that sit. "You've been the mechanism for thirty-four LLC registrations. You're the name on the registered agent filings. You're the California connection in a structure that's otherwise insulated in Nevada. If this becomes a legal proceeding, you're the most accessible point of contact for anyone who wants to establish what the structure was for."

"I registered LLCs," he said. "That's a standard professional function."

"It is. And the analysis of whether registering those specific LLCs constitutes professional participation in the mechanism they were used for — that analysis is what opposing counsel will conduct, in discovery, over approximately eighteen months, using your files and your correspondence and your billing records." I paused. "The organization will have its own counsel. That counsel's obligation is to the organization. Your obligation, in that proceeding, is to yourself."

He set down the mug. "What do you want."

"I want to understand the CivicBridge engagement. Specifically: the consulting agreement between CivicBridge Solutions and a policy advisory practice called DataBridge Consulting, or any affiliated entity, in the period before CivicBridge was incorporated."

The specific quality of how he received that question — the fractional containment of a man who had been anticipating several possible questions and had just been asked the one he hadn't wanted to be asked — told me the document existed. The containment was the tell. If the question had been wrong, the response would have been the ease of relief. What I saw instead was the management of something that required management.

"I have attorney-client privilege on client communications," he said.

"You do. What I'm describing is a consulting agreement that would have been a third-party document passing through your office in the course of entity formation work. Third-party documents in your files are not covered by attorney-client privilege simply because they passed through your office." I let him understand that I knew the distinction. "I'm not asking you to violate privilege. I'm asking you to tell me whether a document exists that I should know exists before I take a different approach to finding it."

He looked at his coffee. At the table. At the parking lot through the window. The complete circuit of avoidances that precede the decision that's already been made.

"There was a framework services agreement," he said. "Between CivicBridge Solutions LLC, as the client, and a consulting entity called Applied Community Analytics, as the service provider. It was executed three months before CivicBridge's incorporation date. It covered what it described as platform architecture and implementation framework services." He paused. "Applied Community Analytics had one listed principal."

"Daniel Voss."

The absence of surprise in my voice was its own communication. He registered it. Something in his posture shifted — not relief, but the specific adjustment of a man who has just confirmed that the person across the table already has what he was deciding whether to give them, and who understands that this changes the calculation.

"The agreement exists in my client formation file for CivicBridge," he said. "It would be discoverable in civil litigation through a properly issued subpoena." He paused. "It would be producible within ten days of such a subpoena."

"That's what I needed to know."

"I need to know—" He stopped. Started again. "I need to know whether the document you've described — whether that document is sufficient for you to—" He couldn't finish the sentence because finishing it required him to name what he was asking for, and naming it was its own kind of exposure.

"It depends on what your counsel advises you about your cooperation posture," I said. "I can't make guarantees about any proceeding I'm not running. What I can tell you is that a prosecutor or a civil litigant reviewing your participation in this matter will evaluate it in light of the choices you make when you become aware of the full scope of the situation." I picked up the coffee. "You're aware of the full scope of the situation."

He nodded. Once. He left before his coffee was finished.

I stayed for a second cup because I'd earned it.


SCENE 4 — THE PARKING LOT CALL
---------------------------------

I called Park from the diner parking lot while the morning traffic on Garey Avenue ran its operations around me — the Pomona version of the city's machine, smaller scale, same function, the same continuous metabolic process of people moving between the places they had to be and the places they needed to be and the places they went to avoid being wherever they were supposed to be.

I told her about the framework services agreement. Applied Community Analytics, sole principal Daniel Voss, executed three months before CivicBridge incorporated, for platform architecture and implementation framework services. She listened with the compressed focus of someone recording every word for later analysis.

"Applied Community Analytics," she repeated.

"Not in any database I've run. Probably registered in a state with minimal disclosure requirements — Delaware, Wyoming."

"Give me the name and I'll have it found." A pause. "The agreement in Mace's file — the subpoena for it goes to Mace directly, or to CivicBridge's records?"

"Mace's client formation file. Mace controls the production." I paused. "He'll produce it under the right circumstances."

"By 'right circumstances' you mean a civil suit in which he's not a named defendant and in which his cooperation can be structured as voluntary disclosure."

"That's what I mean."

"That depends on how we construct the complaint." She was quiet for a moment. Not processing — working. "If we file the civil suit first, before the AG proceeding, we control the discovery architecture. We name Kingdom Cares and CivicBridge as defendants. We don't name Mace. We issue his subpoena in the first thirty days. He produces the Voss agreement in the first sixty." Another pause. "The civil complaint buys us a different timeline than the AG referral. Faster in some dimensions, slower in others."

"What's the fast dimension."

"Discovery. Once we file, they have to respond. They can't close our authentication expert through a consulting conflict because we've already opened the record through the court process." She paused. "The problem is that filing the civil complaint requires a named plaintiff with standing and documented harm. We need someone whose injury is provable, identifiable, and willing to be named."

I thought about Marisol Reyes, whose lease irregularity notice was sitting on her kitchen table. Whose insulin had been deactivated and reinstated as a demonstration of capability. Whose name was in a secondary flag protocol due in four months, now possibly accelerated.

"I may have someone," I said. "I need to have a conversation first."

"Have it today," Park said. "We're not moving on three months anymore. We're moving on whatever timeline Sarah Chu has."

I thanked her. Sat in the parking lot for another minute. The circuit was watching Mace's decisions. Voss was watching the case architecture through the deal channel. Park was building the legal structure from the evidence up. Raoul was managing the human timeline through chalk marks and community resources. Sarah was moving every night.

And Marisol Reyes was on the other end of the Westlake bus line, with a landlord notice in fourteen days and an insulin access she'd learned to confirm every morning because she'd learned that confirmation was not redundant.

I drove to Westlake.
