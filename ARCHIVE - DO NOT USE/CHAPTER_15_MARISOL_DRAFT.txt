
CHAPTER 15: MARISOL


The elevator on the Alvarado building was slow again. Back to its ordinary velocity, which was the velocity of a machine that had been performing its function since the Carter administration and had reached an understanding with time: it would continue operating, and time would continue not asking it to hurry. I took it because I was carrying the case file in a manila envelope and because the stairs were for when I needed the seconds, and this morning I needed Marisol to see me arriving without urgency, the way a person arrived when the news they were carrying was serious but not immediate.

She'd seen me through the door viewer before I knocked. I could tell by the absence of the chain sound — she'd unchained the door before opening it, which she'd done on the first visit too, which told me something about her assessment of me that I didn't take lightly and tried to deserve.

The lease irregularity notice was on the kitchen table. I'd expected it on the counter, or the shelf, somewhere she'd put things she wasn't sure what to do with. It was on the table because she'd been looking at it. Reading it again. The way you read something you already understood, to see if it still said what it said.

I picked it up. Read it. It said what it said.

The landlord's letter was careful language — legal-adjacent, not legal, written to communicate concern and require response without creating a documentable eviction notice. It referenced a prior tenancy associated with her application that showed a name discrepancy in a third-party screening report. It asked for clarification and supporting documentation within fourteen days. It did not threaten. It didn't need to. The threat was in the structure: fourteen days, supporting documentation for a discrepancy that had been manufactured inside a system she'd never accessed and had no mechanism to correct, which meant whatever she submitted would be insufficient and the insufficiency would generate the next letter.

She was watching me read it.

"You know what this is," she said. Not a question.

"I know what it is," I said. "I also know what to do with it."

I put the notice down carefully — not dismissively, the way you put down something you were going to need — and sat across from her. Outside, the Alvarado building did its morning operations: someone in the hallway, a door, the elevator cable moving in its shaft. The ordinary infrastructure of a building full of people who had their own lease irregularity notices in various states of materialization, most of which they would never have the information to recognize for what they were.

"I need to ask you something," I said. "And I need you to hear the whole thing before you answer."


I told her what being a named plaintiff meant. I used plain language because she deserved plain language and because the plain-language version was accurate and the legalized version was accurate in the way that deliberately complicated language was accurate: technically, at the expense of the thing it was describing.

A named plaintiff was a person whose name appeared on the civil complaint — the document that said: these defendants did these things to this person and this person is asking the court to require them to stop and to compensate the harm they caused. Her name would be on a public filing. Anyone could see it. The defendants would see it. The defendants' attorneys would research her extensively — her employment history, her financial records, her prior interactions with the legal system, everything that could be used to complicate her credibility as a witness. She would be deposed, which meant attorneys for the other side would ask her questions under oath and she would need to answer them accurately. The process would take months, possibly more than a year.

She said: "What happens to the other people."

I told her about the 312 active protocols. The secondary flag timeline for three families Raoul was currently managing. The full scope of the Continuity Management system as Sarah Chu had documented it and as the civil complaint would describe it. The named plaintiff's case was the door. What came through the door, once it opened, was larger than any individual harm.

She said: "What happens to me if you lose."

I told her that too. If the civil suit failed — if the evidence wasn't sufficient or the chain was successfully challenged or the court ruled in the defendants' favor — her public association with the complaint would remain on record, and the Circuit would have the legal system's imprimatur on its own conduct, and her position would be materially worse than it was now. I didn't minimize it. She hadn't asked me to minimize it.

She was quiet for a moment. Looking at the pill organizer on the counter, which had a different relevance now than it had had on my first visit — not a medical object but a record, the daily ledger of a woman who had been managing precarity for eighteen months and had developed the specific discipline of checking every day whether the stability she'd been told was permanent was still there.

"The people who designed this," she said. "They used our data. They used the information we gave to the programs that were supposed to help us."

"Yes."

"And they used it to decide who was a problem for their real estate deals."

"Yes."

She looked at me directly. "I was a problem," she said. "I saw their men in a yard and I wrote a letter about it. That's why I'm in the system."

"That's why you're in the system."

She nodded once. Not resolving something — confirming it. "I've been a problem before," she said. "For systems that needed me to not see things. I'm still here." She straightened the pill organizer. The small physical adjustment of a person who had made a decision and was reorienting to its consequences. "What do I sign."

I told her she'd need to meet with Park before any signing. She agreed. I called Park from the kitchen while Marisol made coffee, and Park picked up before the second ring because she'd been waiting for this call since I'd left the Pomona parking lot.



I drove to Santa Monica from Westlake because I'd been sitting on the Julie problem long enough that sitting on it had become its own kind of action — the action of letting the thing build pressure rather than directing it, and the pressure had been building since the moment I'd found her name in the Continuity Management system and it was now at the level where not acting was a worse choice than acting wrong.

I didn't call ahead. The call was the problem. Four rings and a voicemail: her honest communication that she'd said what she needed to say in the last message, and the call was the wrong medium for what came next, and I'd been slow to understand that. The medium for what came next was paper. Paper was her territory — she'd grown up in a household where the complicated things came in letters, written by a man who was better at prose than at presence, and even if that was not a legacy I was proud of having installed in her, I had installed it, and I could use it now.

I parked on the street a block from her building. Sat in the car and wrote by hand on the back of a manila envelope because I hadn't brought paper, which was an oversight consistent with my methodology for the past three weeks: always slightly behind the preparation the moment required, always making do with what was available, and finding that what was available was usually sufficient if you used it correctly.

The note was four sentences.

Your name is in a database called Continuity Management, operated by a company called CivicBridge Solutions. You appear as a proximity marker attached to your organization's profile — you're not a target, but you're being watched. Your phones and professional communications may be monitored. Don't change your behavior — acting normally is what keeps the marker passive — but if you notice anything anomalous in your accounts or communications, call this number.

I wrote Raoul's community-resource number at the bottom — not mine, because mine was already in the database, and Raoul's was clean.

I folded it. Wrote her name on the outside. Walked to the building. Her mailboxes were in the lobby, and the lobby door was open during the day. I put the envelope through the mail slot for unit 304, listened to it hit the floor on the other side, and walked back to my car.

Sat for a moment.

The lamp was on in the third-floor corner window. It was 2 p.m. — she was working. The lamp during the day meant something different than the lamp at night: not the infrastructure of a woman working late, but a woman who needed the light to work, who had not changed this habit in the thirty years since the Pasadena house, and who would come home this evening to an envelope on the floor of her mailbox that would tell her that the light she kept on was in a database somewhere, catalogued and weighted and maintained as a risk indicator by a system she'd done nothing to enter.

I started the car. Drove east.

I did not look back because I'd committed to not looking back since I'd put the envelope through the slot, and commitment had to be complete or it wasn't commitment.


She called at 11:23 p.m.

I was at the desk, working through the motion drafts Park had sent for review, and the phone lit up with her name — not the voicemail notification but the call itself, live, the screen asking: do you want to answer this question. I answered it.

"Dad." The compression codec, the familiar flatness of a medium that handled her voice the way all digital media handled human voices: adequately, and no more. "I got your note."

"I know."

"The number at the bottom. I looked it up — it's a community resource contact. Not yours."

"Mine is in the database I described. I didn't want you using a compromised channel to reach me about a compromised channel."

A pause. The specific pause of a person absorbing a sentence that was both technically precise and personally significant — that contained information and also contained a version of the man who had sent it, and the two things required separate processing. "How long has my name been in it."

"Two years."

Another pause. "The organization I work for."

"Yes. The digital rights work flagged a monitoring interest in 2022. Your name appeared as a proximity marker when they logged the organization." A pause. "You haven't been targeted. The marker is passive unless something activates it. I'm building the case that takes the system down. The marker goes with it."

"How long until the case is—"

"I don't know. Weeks. Possibly a month. The civil suit was filed today." I paused. "There's a named plaintiff. The evidence is in shape. The timeline is compressed."

The silence this time was longer. Not processing — something else. "You've been in this for a while," she said.

"Since the beginning of the month."

"The voicemail I sent." Her voice changed register — not to the softer place where Julie put things that cost her, but to the precise concrete place, which was its own kind of cost. "The one where I said I couldn't have you in my life without it costing something I don't have left."

"I have it. I've listened to it."

"I meant it," she said. "And I also—" She stopped. "I don't know how to say the thing I want to say without it sounding like I'm changing position."

"You don't have to say it tonight."

"No." The specific firmness of a person who had decided this was the conversation and was going to have it. "I meant the thing I said. The loop is real. The cost is real. And I also—" A pause. "I also checked the drawer tonight when I found the note and I still have every letter you've ever written me. Including the ones from when I was seven and you were away on the Heartland thing and you wrote one a week even when there was nothing to say." A pause. "You've always been better at paper."

I didn't say anything. There wasn't a sentence that wouldn't cost more than the silence.

"Are you safe," she said.

"I'm being careful."

"That's not the same thing."

"No," I said. "It isn't."

"Okay." The specific compressed-codec version of okay — insufficient to carry what it was doing, but carrying it anyway. "Don't stop being careful."

"I won't."

She ended the call. I sat with the phone in my hand for a long moment. Then I put it face-up on the desk — not face-down, the way I usually put it when it had just delivered something difficult. Face-up. I wanted to know if it rang again.

It didn't.

I went back to the motion drafts.
