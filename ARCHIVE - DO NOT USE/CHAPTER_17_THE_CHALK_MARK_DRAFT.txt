CHAPTER 17: CHALK MARK

Raoul's instructions for the chalk-mark protocol were precise the way operational instructions were always precise when imprecision was a risk: specific location, specific marking, specific secondary drop, specific timing window. I'd memorized them from his text and deleted the text, because anything that existed in readable form existed in a system that the Circuit had demonstrated the capacity to access.

The mark was on a section of sidewalk on Figueroa at the south end of a Metro station entrance — a diagonal chalk line, white, left of the handicapped ramp's edge, oriented northeast. The kind of mark that read as incidental debris to anyone who hadn't been told it was something else. I'd walked past the station three times in the past two days looking for it, because Raoul had said the mark would appear when Sarah was ready rather than on a schedule, and Sarah's readiness was a variable I didn't control.

It was there on Saturday morning.

I walked past it without slowing. Noted it. Continued south for two blocks, turned east, and counted to the secondary location: a newspaper box on the corner of Figueroa and a numbered cross street, the kind of box that sold a weekly circular that nobody read but that had been in that corner for fifteen years and had achieved the invisibility of permanent fixtures. Under the box, in the gap between the box's base and the curb, a folded piece of paper inside a small plastic bag.

The paper had one line in Sarah's handwriting: ECHO PARK LAKE, NORTH SIDE, 11 A.M. BENCH 3 FROM THE EAST PATH ENTRANCE. BRING NOTHING YOU CAN'T WALK AWAY FROM.

I read it twice. Burned it — a lighter I kept for paper, not for smoking, in an empty parking structure stairwell where the smoke would disperse. Walked back to my car.

Echo Park Lake. In the open. No building, no enclosed space, no interior that could be monitored or cleared. She'd adapted the meeting methodology the same way she'd adapted the location methodology when the gap categories failed: when the enclosed space became dangerous, you moved to the open space and used its public density as cover. A bench at a public lake on a Saturday morning was invisible precisely because it was visible — a man and a woman sitting on a bench, in the view of fifty people, doing nothing that registered as anything other than what it appeared to be.

I drove home, changed into something that belonged at a Saturday lake, and left the .38 in the desk drawer. She'd said bring nothing you can't walk away from, and the .38 was something I wouldn't walk away from. I put it in the drawer with the specific discomfort of a man leaving behind the thing he trusted most. Noted the discomfort. Left it.

The lake at 11 a.m. on a Saturday had the quality of a public space that had been reclaimed, fought over, and reclaimed again — the specific tired vitality of a place that had been through several versions of itself in a short period and was currently in the version where it was simply a lake again, doing what lakes did, which was providing water and birds and a perimeter for walking and a bench situation that suited everyone from the person feeding ducks to the person meeting a contact in an investigation. The ducks were present and active. A man was flying a kite that had no business being flown near a lake but was doing it with the cheerful incompetence of someone enjoying himself, which was its own kind of cover.

I found Bench 3 and sat at the east end of it. Two minutes.

She came from the north path — I saw her before she reached the bench because I was watching the north path — and she was wearing the kind of non-memorable clothing that communicated she'd been thinking about how to be seen for a long time. Not a disguise. The absence of anything that would anchor a description. She sat at the west end of the bench with a gap between us, which meant we were two people sharing a bench rather than two people meeting, and she looked at the lake with the calm of someone who had learned to make inactivity look intentional.

"You filed," she said.

"Yesterday. How did you know."

"I have a search alert set on the federal civil filing system for Kingdom Cares. It triggered last night." She paused. "The named plaintiff. Is she all right."

"She said yes when she understood what it meant. She's been waiting for a mechanism."

Sarah nodded, looking at the lake. Her face had the quality I'd seen in Echo Park — the navigational calm of someone who had been managing continuous threat long enough to have stopped experiencing it as threat and started experiencing it as terrain. But the terrain was harder now than it had been three weeks ago. The chalk-mark schedule was in the face.

"There's a program director," she said. "At a re-entry services organization called Pathway Home. They're one of the thirty-one partners in the Continuity Management network. Her name is Devin Cole — she's been running their re-entry program for four years and she contacted me three weeks ago after she found something in their data integration interface that she didn't recognize and that didn't match the documentation they'd been given for their CivicBridge platform access."

"She found the Continuity Management module."

"She found a data output field that was being populated by her client intake records and sending to an external endpoint that wasn't in their data-sharing agreement. She followed the endpoint. It led to a CivicBridge API that wasn't in any of their contracted service documentation." She paused. "She didn't know what it was. She knew it wasn't what she'd agreed to. She contacted me because she'd found my name in the organization's prior incident notes — Beacon Forward had her on a contact list before I left."

"She can testify."

"She will testify. She has the technical documentation — the API endpoint, the data flow records, the discrepancy between the contracted service documentation and the actual data transmission." She looked at me directly. "Her intake records have been transmitting to the Continuity Management system for four years. Every client she's helped through Pathway Home has a profile in the flag registry. She wants to know what happened to them."

I thought about Raoul's twenty-three properties. The 312 active protocols. Elaine Booth's seven years of briefcase documentation. Devin Cole's four years of intake records feeding the machine without her knowledge. Each person who had found the thread from a different angle, in a different city, over a different timeline, and had been carrying it alone because there was no system for carrying it together.

The civil suit was the system.

"I need to get her to Park," I said.

"She'll go. She's been waiting for something to go to." Sarah paused. "Give me a contact method for Park that doesn't route through your number or mine."

I gave her Raoul's community-resource line. She would route Cole to Park through Raoul, which put three relay points between Cole and any monitoring of our communication channels. It was slower than direct contact. It was the speed the evidence could survive at.


I called Park from the car on the way home. Cole first, then the county motion. She took the Cole information with the compressed efficiency of someone updating a complex architecture in real time. "Four-year data transmission records from a legitimate nonprofit partner," she said. "That's not circumstantial evidence of the mechanism. That's operational documentation of the mechanism produced by one of the mechanism's own partners. A jury sees that and they don't need the academic journal article."

"She'll come through Raoul."

"I'll be ready." A pause. "The county motion — I filed it yesterday as we discussed. The judge has calendared a preliminary review session for Thursday. The county has until Wednesday to respond to the federal jurisdiction question." She paused. "There's something else. The defendants' attorneys filed a motion this morning — not the motion to dismiss, something preliminary. A motion for an order requiring the plaintiff to identify and preserve all communications with the investigator conducting the underlying investigation."

"They want my files."

"They want a court order requiring you to preserve and potentially produce your investigation files as part of the defendants' discovery. It's a legitimate discovery motion but the timing is aggressive — filing it within twenty-four hours of service means they had the motion drafted before the complaint was filed." A pause. "If the court grants it, your files — everything you've gathered since the beginning of the case — become subject to the defendants' discovery requests."

I thought about the air-gapped machine in the office. The fourteen chapters of the case file. The Beacon Forward extraction on the USB drive. The photographs from Marisol's folder. The journal article. Elaine Booth's briefcase. The conference photograph of Voss and Kingston. The note in Raoul's handwriting with Mace's name on it.

"My files include intelligence about the AG's concurrent investigation," I said. "If those files are produced in discovery, the Circuit's attorneys can identify Reyes as the AG contact and deduce the investigation's shape."

"I know." Park was quiet for a moment. "I'm filing an opposition to the motion today. The argument: your files are protected work product of a consulting investigator retained by counsel in anticipation of litigation. Work product doctrine. It's defensible. It's not certain." She paused. "We're in the period that's the most dangerous part. I told you that when you filed."

"I know."

"Keep your files secure. Don't move them to any cloud storage or networked device."

The air-gapped machine. The epoxy in the wireless port. I'd built it for exactly this possibility. "They're secure."

"Good." She paused. "Thursday, the preliminary review session. Be available. If the judge wants to hear from the investigator directly about the work product designation, you may need to appear."

I said I would. She ended the call.

I drove east through the Saturday city. It was doing the Saturday version of itself — more foot traffic, the sidewalk economy active in the way it was active on days when people had discretionary time, which meant the city's enormous machine was displaying its consumer-facing surface, the bright-colored version that it used to cover the operational layer that ran continuously beneath it. I'd stopped seeing the surface version some time around year three of this work. I saw the operational layer now by default. The surface version seemed, when I noticed it at all, like the kind of optimism that required not looking carefully at what you were being optimistic about.

Which was most optimism. That was fine. The city needed people who could live on the surface. I just wasn't one of them anymore.


Raoul's check-in time was 6 p.m. I had a protocol with him: a daily confirmation, one text to a shared secure number, a code word that changed weekly. If the 6 p.m. message didn't arrive by 6:15, I called. If the call didn't connect within three attempts, I took specific actions.

6 p.m. Nothing.

6:15. I called. Three attempts. No connection.

I sat at the desk with the .38 back in my waistband — I'd put it back when I'd come home from Echo Park, the desk drawer's discomfort resolved as soon as the meeting was over and the terrain reverted to operational — and thought through the possible explanations in order of likelihood. Phone dead or lost: possible, one in five. Raoul in a situation where contact was impossible: one in five. Something wrong with the secure number infrastructure: one in ten. The Circuit.

The remaining probability sat in the desk with the bourbon I wasn't drinking.

I called the secondary contact — a woman in El Sereno connected to the Inland Solidarity Collective, the front-door infrastructure through which I'd originally reached Raoul. She answered on the second ring. When I said Raoul's name she was quiet for two seconds longer than a person who had nothing to tell me would have been quiet.

"He was doing a welfare check," she said. "One of the families on his watch list. The family in Boyle Heights — their secondary flag was due."

"When did he go."

"This afternoon. Around three." A pause. "He hasn't come back. He hasn't called. He doesn't do that."

Three hours. A welfare check that should take forty minutes had produced a three-hour silence from a man who understood operational security well enough to have maintained it for three years.

I got the Boyle Heights address from her. Drove.

The building was a two-story apartment complex three blocks from the freeway — the kind of building that had served its residential function through multiple neighborhood transitions by being structurally sound and affordable and positioned near transit that made it useful to people who worked at distance from where they lived. The lights were on in the second-floor unit I'd been given. There was a truck in the parking lot that I recognized: Raoul's 2014 Ford, registered to the P.O. box in El Sereno, parked in the lot with the parking lights on.

Not the engine. The parking lights. The specific configuration of a vehicle that had been left in a state that communicated: someone left in a hurry, or someone left without choosing to leave.

I sat in my car for sixty seconds. Did the assessment. Went in.

The building's exterior access was unlocked. Second floor. The apartment door was closed but the frame showed the specific mark of a forced entry that had been quickly repaired — someone had pushed past a chain lock and re-engaged it from the outside to make the door look undisturbed. I tested the door. The chain held. I tested the chain. It had been engaged from the outside, which was possible but required a tool or a specific technique and left a trace in the chrome of the chain's receiver.

I knocked. Nothing. Knocked again, the specific sequence from Raoul's protocol.

From inside: a sound. A single knock, knee height. The knock I'd heard once before, from the other side of a door in Echo Park.

Not Raoul. Sarah's protocol.

But from inside Raoul's welfare check address.

I went to the building's super, who was on the ground floor and who opened her door with the resigned expression of a building manager who had seen various categories of unexpected visitors over the years. I told her there might be a gas smell from 2B. She produced a master key with the speed of someone who had been trained on gas-smell protocols.

The apartment was occupied: a family — a woman in her forties, two children I guessed at twelve and eight, sitting in the kitchen with the controlled stillness of people who had been told to be still. Raoul was on the floor in the living room with his back against the couch, a cut above his eye that had been cleaned and bandaged with materials from a first aid kit on the coffee table, and the careful breathing of a man managing pain he was not willing to display.

He looked up when I came in. His expression was the specific economy of a man who was managing multiple assessments simultaneously — his own physical state, the family's state, my reaction to his state, what my reaction communicated about the next move.

"Two men," he said. "They were here when I arrived. They'd already spoken to Elena." He nodded toward the woman at the kitchen table. "They said the lease review was being resolved at the property level. They said cooperation with external parties investigating the property owner would complicate the resolution." He paused. "When I asked them to identify themselves, one of them provided a business card for a property management company. The other one said nothing and communicated his position through other means."

The cut above his eye.

"The card," I said.

He reached into his jacket. The property management company on the card was WESTERN LAND SERVICES, INC.

I looked at the card. Looked at Raoul. Looked at Elena and her children at the kitchen table, sitting with the composed immobility of people who had learned to be very still when men came to talk about their lease.

Something had changed. The Circuit had crossed the line it had been maintaining since the first wiper-blade card — from the administrative mechanism into a direct physical presence in the homes of the people on the suppression list. From the patient architecture of a system that processed people without leaving fingerprints into something that left a cut above Raoul Cruz's eye and a property management business card on a coffee table.

This was what happens next is not under his management looked like.

I took a photograph of the business card. Photographed Raoul's eye. Called Park and told her to draft an emergency motion for a temporary restraining order — defendants and their agents, named and unnamed, from making contact with the plaintiff and any person involved in the plaintiff's case. She said she'd file in the morning. I said tonight.

She filed at 11:47 p.m.

I stayed in the Boyle Heights apartment until 2 a.m., until the family had settled and Raoul had assessed that his rib was bruised rather than broken and had accepted the tea that Elena made him without making anything of it — the specific transaction between people who understood that care offered in difficult circumstances was care that mattered and was not sentimental, it was operational — and the city ran its overnight maintenance protocol outside, feeding, humming, converting the night's events into morning's administrative record.

Western Land Services.

Not administrative variance. Not a service deactivation. A business card delivered by a man who had communicated his position through other means.

The fire was starting.
