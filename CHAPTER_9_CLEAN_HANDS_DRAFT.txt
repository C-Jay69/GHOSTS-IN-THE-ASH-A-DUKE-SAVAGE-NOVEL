CHAPTER 9: CLEAN HANDS


Her office was in a Koreatown building that had survived four decades of the city's various enthusiasms by being structurally sound and aesthetically unconcerned, which was the architecture of longevity in Los Angeles. The elevator was slow and functional and did not communicate anything about the people who used it. The hallway smelled like industrial cleaner and the particular kind of ambition that expressed itself through paper rather than performance — filing cabinets through open doors, stacked depositions, the physical weight of accumulated argument.

Joanna Park had been litigating civil rights cases against municipal and county agencies for nineteen years, which meant she had spent nineteen years doing the specific work of finding the places where the law's language and the institution's behavior had been pulled far enough apart that the gap became a claim. She was fifty-one, with the compressed focused energy of someone who slept fewer hours than was medically recommended because the alternative was leaving something unfinished, and she had the quality I associated with people who had won enough to know exactly why they'd lost the ones they hadn't: a precise understanding of where the evidence ended and the argument began, and a precise intolerance for confusing the two.

She looked at what I'd given her for a long time without speaking.

The CivicBridge architecture printout. The Continuity Management module documentation. The partial active protocol list. The chain of custody narrative I'd written at 2 a.m. — a clean account of how each piece of evidence had been obtained, by whom, through what methods, in what sequence. The chain was honest. That was what made it complicated.

"The extraction," she said. "Sarah Chu. She had legitimate user credentials for the Beacon Forward database."

"She was a data analyst. The system access was part of her job function."

"She extracted records that went beyond her job function's scope." Park turned a page. "Which means we have data that was obtained by someone who had authorized access to the system but exceeded the authorized scope of that access. California Penal Code 502 covers unauthorized computer access, which has been interpreted broadly by some courts to include scope exceedance." She paused. "If opposing counsel wants to challenge the admissibility of the extraction, they have a road to try."

"She was trying to expose a suppression operation running through her employer's system."

"Which is a compelling narrative and inadmissible as a defense to the chain of custody problem." She looked at me. "I'm not dismissing it. I'm telling you what they'll do. The Circuit's lawyers — whoever they are — will make the chain the issue, because if the chain falls, the evidence falls, and if the evidence falls, we have nothing but a story. Stories don't create injunctions."

"What do you need."

She produced a legal pad and a pen with the efficiency of someone who had already been thinking about this since our phone call. "One: Sarah Chu's cooperation. She needs to provide a declaration — under penalty of perjury, in her own name — describing the discovery process, the reasons she extracted the data, and the methods she used. Signed, notarized, timestamped. If she's credible and if her access was within reasonable scope under the circumstances, a court can find the extraction was made in good faith. It's not a guarantee. It's the best version of this argument."

"She's in a gap-category building. She doesn't have a fixed address or a way to receive legal documents."

"Then find her. That's your job." She wrote something on the pad. "Two: the verbal encryption key. You received an encryption key to the drive verbally, from a source in an unsecured location, with no documentation. The drive's content is therefore authenticated by what exactly?"

"By the content itself, which corresponds independently to documents obtained through public-record searches, Marisol Reyes's testimony, and Gerald Mace's LLC registrations — all of which I obtained through clean methods."

She nodded, writing. "Which establishes corroboration, not authentication. They're related but not the same. We need a technical expert who can examine the drive's metadata, the timestamps on the files, and testify that the files are what they purport to be and were not created or modified after the fact." She wrote something else. "I know someone at UC Irvine. Three: Victoria Cross."

"What about her."

"She's your chain of custody problem wearing a human face. She's an admitted former Circuit employee who orchestrated the engagement of the investigator who found the evidence. If she testifies, opposing counsel will spend every minute of cross-examination establishing that the entire investigation was designed by a person with motive to frame the Circuit." Park looked up. "Is there a version of this where she doesn't testify?"

"She has inside knowledge of the suppression protocol's operation. She executed Sarah Chu's suppression sequence herself — she changed the four data points. That's direct evidence."

"It's also a confession to having participated in the very thing we're prosecuting. Which a good defense attorney will use to contaminate the entire narrative." She put the pen down. "I'm not saying she can't testify. I'm saying her testimony needs to be the last thing the court hears about Victoria Cross, not the first thing it hears about the case. We build the architecture first. We build it from evidence that doesn't depend on her. Then, when the structure is already standing, we add her testimony as confirmation."

"How long."

"To build a case this could survive? Three months. Possibly four."

Sarah Chu had been in her third gap-category location for less than twenty-four hours. Raoul had told me the viable gap status of a location lasted weeks, not months. There was a timeline problem that Park's legal architecture could not resolve.

"The AG contact," I said. "Victoria gave me a name."

Park looked at me with the specific expression of a lawyer who was about to tell me something they'd been waiting to tell me. "Call the AG contact before you do anything else. I've told you what I need. Call them today. The reason matters."

She wouldn't say more. I filed the instruction.


SCENE 2 — THE AG COMPLICATION
--------------------------------

The contact's name was Reyes — no relation to Marisol, which was the first thing I checked and confirmed, because in a case this architecturally circular a coincidence of names would have been information. He was a deputy AG in the civil rights enforcement division, and he picked up his own phone, which told me either that he was old-fashioned or that he'd been waiting for the call, and the three seconds of silence after I identified myself told me it was the latter.

"Victoria Cross," he said.

"She said you've been building something on Kingdom Cares."

"For seven months." A pause that had the texture of something being decided. "I need you to understand the situation before we go any further. What I'm building is a county contract fraud investigation — specifically, whether Kingdom Cares misrepresented its data governance practices in its applications for three city and county data-sharing contracts. It's narrow. It's targeted. And it is currently in a phase where any external investigation that overlaps with our subject matter creates a jurisdictional question my supervising AG would need to adjudicate before I can share anything with you."

"You can't talk to me."

"I can talk to you. I can't share investigative materials. And I need to tell you something that you should already know but may not." He paused again. The pause this time had a different quality — not a decision, a preparation. "Kingdom Cares has a legal team. They know we're investigating. They have known for approximately three months, which is longer than I would have preferred, and the person who told them is someone in my office whose name I will not share with you because I don't know it yet and because naming unconfirmed suspicions to an investigator I've never met is not something I'm willing to do." He let that land. "What I am willing to tell you is that anything I receive, anything I touch in my official capacity, is potentially visible to people I don't trust. Do you understand what I'm telling you."

"The channel is compromised."

"I'm telling you to be careful what you send through the channel."

"Victoria's been working you for eighteen months," I said.

A silence. "Longer."

"She knows about the leak."

"Almost certainly. She's been building her approach around the assumption that anything that goes through official channels goes through that leak." Another pause. "Which is why she needed someone outside official channels to build the evidence architecture. And why that architecture needs to arrive through the attorney general's office in a form that doesn't require me to have received it through my standard intake process." He said it the way you said something when you'd been wanting to say it for a while and the moment had finally arrived. "What Park needs and what I need are probably the same thing. I'd suggest you talk to her."

"I just came from her office."

"Then you know what it takes." He paused. "And you know the timeline. Three months is not a timeline that Sarah Chu has."

I thanked him. He ended the call without pleasantries, which was itself a kind of professional communication: this conversation did not happen, and the absence of a goodbye was the mechanism that made it true.

I sat in my car in the Koreatown parking structure and thought about a leak inside the attorney general's office and a timeline of three months and a woman in a former community health clinic in Echo Park whose backpack was packed for forty-five-second departures and whose mother's name was Linda and who lived in Alhambra and who didn't know what her daughter had been doing for eight months. I thought about the gap between what the law could use and what the truth was, which was the gap I'd been living in for nineteen years and which had not gotten smaller.

Then I drove to my office, because someone was waiting there who I hadn't invited.


SCENE 3 — FENN
----------------

He was in the hallway outside my office door — not inside, which told me he either couldn't pick a lock or had decided that being found in the hallway was a more effective communication than being found inside. He was leaning against the wall with the complete physical ease of a man who was comfortable in hallways that weren't his, which was its own kind of credential. Late forties, the same institutional-security build as the man I'd seen visit Mace in Pomona but with a different quality — Pomona had been supervision. This was something with more patience in it.

He looked at me the way people looked at someone they'd been studying for a while and had formed an accurate enough picture of that the in-person confirmation was a minor update rather than a revelation.

"Savage," he said.

"You're Fenn."

If my knowing his name registered, it didn't register on his face. He had the specific face of someone who had been operating in environments where his name was occasionally known by people he hadn't introduced himself to, and who had learned not to treat the information as significant. "You want to have this conversation in the hall or inside."

"Hall's fine," I said. I didn't open the door.

He nodded, slightly, as if the refusal confirmed something he'd expected. "I want to be accurate about why I'm here. I'm not here to tell you to stop. I've looked at what you've built and I've looked at what you have, and telling you to stop would be inaccurate — not because I couldn't make a credible case for the costs, but because you're past the point where stopping changes the calculus, and I don't waste precise communications on imprecise conclusions."

"You're here to price the cost of continuing."

"I'm here to make sure you understand what continuing means for the people connected to you." He said it without inflection, which was the correct choice — inflection would have made it a threat, and it wasn't a threat, it was an accounting. "The woman in Westlake. Her secondary flag timeline is four months. That timeline is administrative, not personal — it's in the system and runs on the system's schedule regardless of anything I do or don't do. I want to be clear about that because accuracy is how I operate."

"But the timeline can be accelerated."

"Administrative timelines can be affected by administrative inputs. Yes." He waited. "The attorney. Park. She's done good work in this city for a long time. Her practice depends on standing in the courts she operates in. Standing depends on reputation. Reputation is a data structure, and data structures can be edited." Another pause. "Again: I'm not telling you this is what will happen. I'm telling you it's what the environment produces when active investigations generate sufficient disruption."

"You're telling me the cost."

"I'm giving you accurate information about the environment. What you do with it is your decision." He straightened from the wall. "The person you should be more concerned about is the one who isn't in this conversation. The one I haven't mentioned."

Julie. He hadn't said the name because he didn't need to and because the kind of man who named the leverage was a different kind of man from Fenn. The naming was the crude version. The not-naming was the architecture.

I looked at him. He looked back with the comfortable patience of someone who had had this category of conversation many times and knew exactly where it went. He was waiting to see which version of the conversation I was going to have: the version where the person he was talking to absorbed the accounting and modified their behavior, which was the most common version, or the other version.

"You're going to tell me something else," I said. "Or you wouldn't be here in person."

A pause that was the first genuine pause — not managed, not part of the communication design. Something had shifted slightly in the parameters of the conversation.

"There's a version of this that ends without the costs I described," he said carefully. "If the investigation closes before it reaches certain levels of the organization. If what's exposed is the mechanism without the model's originating architecture." He paused. "If what reaches the AG is Gerald Mace and CivicBridge and the LLC network. Not the design layer."

"You're telling me there's a deal."

"I'm telling you the environment has a version that produces fewer consequences for the people connected to you." He met my eyes. "I'm also telling you I'm not the person who sends me. And I can't tell you who that person is. I can tell you they didn't know I was coming today, which means this conversation is happening because I decided it should, based on my assessment of the situation."

He held my gaze for a moment longer, then pushed off from the wall and walked to the stairwell.

I stood in the hallway for a moment.

Fenn had come not because the Circuit had sent him. He'd come because Fenn had decided to come, which meant there was something in Fenn's assessment of the situation that the Circuit's instructions hadn't accounted for. A circuit employee who went off-protocol was information. The information was: something in the case had made Fenn believe the standard operating costs were insufficient as a deterrent, which meant the case was producing more pressure on the system than the system had anticipated when it had allowed me to run. I filed it.

Then I went inside and checked the cylinder and thought about the architect.


SCENE 4 — THE LEGACY PROTOCOL
--------------------------------

The CivicBridge internal documentation that Sarah had extracted from the Beacon Forward system included, in the technical architecture files, a series of references I'd noted in my first pass and deferred for later processing. Later had arrived.

The references were to a prior framework: LEGACY CONTINUITY PROTOCOL — LCP — cited in CivicBridge's development notes as the "foundational architecture from which the current platform was derived," with a parenthetical note giving a development date of 2014 and a geographic designation: EASTERN REGION PILOT.

2014 was three years before CivicBridge was incorporated. The model had existed before the company. Someone had designed the LCP in 2014, run it somewhere in an eastern region as a pilot, and then — after three years of what the development notes called "iterative implementation refinement" — had built CivicBridge as the platform to deploy it at scale in Los Angeles.

The architect wasn't in Los Angeles. The architect had built this somewhere else first.

I ran the phrase LEGACY CONTINUITY PROTOCOL through every available search channel — the usual databases, the citation archives, the regulatory filing systems, the nonprofit disclosure databases. Nothing. The phrase had left no public-facing record, which meant the eastern region pilot had been run in an environment with complete institutional insulation, or it had been run at a scale small enough to avoid generating the administrative residue that public-facing operations produced, or the record had been cleaned.

I ran EASTERN REGION PILOT against known Ashworth Capital investments. The fund had been investing since 2011. Its pre-2017 investments were in a different category from the post-2017 portfolio: earlier rounds in municipal technology companies — software platforms for government service delivery, data integration tools for social service agencies, predictive analytics applications for housing assistance programs. All of them in the Northeastern United States.

I ran the Northeastern municipal technology investments forward in time. Three had been acquired or shut down between 2015 and 2017. One had been rebranded. One — a company called Pathway Analytics, headquartered in Baltimore, which had built a community service data integration platform for use by city social service agencies — had gone quiet in 2016. No dissolution filing. No acquisition announcement. No public documentation of what had happened to it.

I ran Pathway Analytics.

Incorporated 2013, Maryland. Initial investor: a philanthropic foundation called MERIDIAN COMMUNITY PARTNERS — a 501(c)(3) whose mission statement described bridge-building between emerging civic technology and vulnerable populations. I looked at the mission statement for a moment with the specific feeling of reading something I'd read before in a different form: it was Beacon Forward's language, three years earlier, different city, different name, same sentence.

Meridian Community Partners had been funded primarily by a single donor: a private family foundation that I'd never heard of. The family foundation's registered agent was a law firm in Baltimore. I'd need more time to unwind the structure — the donor disclosure requirements for private foundations were even thinner than for 501(c)(3)s, and whoever had set this up had set it up with the same care that Gerald Mace applied to Nevada LLC registrations.

But I had the shape of it now.

The architect had built the model in Baltimore in 2013. Run the pilot under the name Legacy Continuity Protocol through Pathway Analytics. Refined it for three years. Come to Los Angeles in 2017 and built CivicBridge as the deployment platform. Had Ashworth's money in both iterations. Had a different foundation name in Baltimore — Meridian Community Partners — versus Kingdom Cares in Los Angeles, but the same sentence in both mission statements, which was either coincidence or the tell of someone who had stopped bothering to vary the language because the language had never been the thing that got them caught.

The architect wasn't Kingston. Kingston was the Los Angeles deployment. The architect was whoever had built Meridian Community Partners in 2013, tested the model in Baltimore with Ashworth's early-round money, and then brought it west.

The case was going to Baltimore.

I looked at the document I'd started the night before: ACT II — WHAT THEY BUILT IT FOR. I typed a new line below the others: LCP — BALTIMORE — MERIDIAN COMMUNITY PARTNERS — 2013. Looked at it. The thread was thin but it was there, the way a bone fragment suggested the skeleton underneath it: specific, located, pointing in a direction.

I thought about Fenn in the hallway. The version of this that ends without the costs I described. If what reaches the AG is the mechanism without the model's originating architecture. The Circuit was willing to lose its Los Angeles components if it could protect the architect. Which meant the architect was more valuable than Kingston, more valuable than CivicBridge, more valuable than the fourteen properties and the 312 active protocols and Gerald Mace's law practice and the gala and the smile.

The architect was the model. If the model survived, the Circuit survived — redeployable, in another city, with another nonprofit name and another land use attorney and another software company and another philanthropist with a $220,000 smile.

I was not going to leave the architect in place.

I opened the Tuesday pill case. Wednesday's slot. I'd been managing the sequence cleanly since the night I'd first opened the file — the omission becoming intention becoming habit, the way things became habit when you'd found a reason for them. I took it. Dry, the way I always took it. The way men committed to things they weren't going to discuss.

Then I started building the Baltimore search architecture.
